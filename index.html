ML Regression for AutoTrader Pricing

Keywords: Supervised Learning, Principal Components Analysis, Scikit-Learn, Pandas, Model Ensembling

In this project, I leveraged machine learning to predict vehicle selling prices using real-world data from AutoTrader. The aim was to build a robust model that could accurately estimate a vehicle's market value based on key features.

Key Highlights

  • Data Cleaning and Feature Engineering: I started by cleaning and preprocessing the dataset, addressing missing values, and engineering features to extract meaningful information.
  • Model Selection: After extensive testing, I identified the Random Forest Regressor as the best-performing model for the task. This powerful algorithm showed superior predictive capabilities.
  • Dimensionality Reduction: To further enhance model performance, I implemented Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for dimensionality reduction, allowing to maintain accuracy while reducing computational complexity.
  • Boosted Trees: I explored Gradient Boosting Regressor and Histogram-Based Gradient Boosting Regressor models with optimized hyperparameters, which provided valuable insights into the dataset.
  • Ensemble Modeling: To boost predictive power, I employed ensemble techniques like Voting and Stacking. These methods combined multiple models to achieve more accurate results.
  • Model Analysis: I delved into model interpretability with techniques like Shap values, partial dependency plots, and permutation importance. These tools helped me understand feature importance and interactions.
  • Performance on the Entire Dataset: The final ensemble model was applied to the complete dataset, showcasing strong performance and generalisation capabilities.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates a comprehensive approach to predictive modeling, emphasizing feature engineering, model selection, and ensemble techniques. It provides valuable insights into the vehicle pricing domain and serves as a foundation for future enhancements and applications.

Deep Learning for Post-Hurricane Damage Assessment

Keywords: PyTorch, Transfer Learning, ImageNet, VGG16, ResNet50

In this project, I utilsed deep learning models, VGG16 and ResNet50, to assess post-hurricane damage in houses using satellite imagery. I trained these models on a dataset of 10,000 images of damaged and undamaged houses from Hurricane Harvey. The results were promising, with VGG16 achieving an accuracy of 80.85% and an F1 score of 0.8341. I also employed image augmentation techniques and performed ROC-AUC analysis to validate the models' effectiveness in predicting damage status.

Key Highlights

  • Development of deep learning models (VGG16 and ResNet50) for post-hurricane damage assessment.
  • Training on a dataset of 10,000 satellite images from Hurricane Harvey.
  • Notable performance with VGG16, reaching an 80.85% accuracy and an F1 score of 0.8341.
  • Effective utilization of image augmentation techniques and ROC-AUC analysis for model evaluation.

Conclusion

Through this project, I successfully demonstrated the potential of deep learning models, especially VGG16, for rapid and accurate post-hurricane damage classification using satellite imagery. These models hold significant promise for aiding emergency response efforts in the aftermath of natural disasters. However, further research and optimization are necessary to enhance model generalization and reduce computational costs for real-world applications.

Analysis of Bike Rides using Apache Spark

Keywords: PySpark, Hypothesis Testing

Advancements in data storage capacity and the reduction in memory device size have paved the way for handling large volumes of data, known as big data. This report explores the use of Apache Spark 3.3.2 to test two hypotheses related to bike rentals in London in 2014. The first hypothesis examines whether people ride longer in autumn than in spring, while the second investigates the relationship between bike rentals and traffic volume.

Key Highlights

  • Utilized Apache Spark 3.3.2 for data analysis and hypothesis testing.
  • Tested two hypotheses related to bike rentals in London in 2014.
  • Found statistically significant differences in bike ride durations between spring and autumn.
  • Explored the relationship between bike rentals and traffic volume.
  • Correlation analysis revealed little correlation between bike rentals and traffic volume.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the analysis of bike rentals in London provided valuable insights. While there is evidence of longer bike ride durations in spring compared to autumn, the relationship between bike rentals and traffic volume remains inconclusive. Further research incorporating geographical data may offer a more accurate representation of traffic patterns and their impact on bike rentals.
